What we are

We make event planning a joy

Events bring people together. That’s a simple idea, but organising one has quietly become one of the hardest jobs inside a modern business: dozens of suppliers to manage, budgets that move weekly and a week of waiting before you know whether a venue is even available. The person running the event is usually doing it on top of another full-time job.

We set out to fix that. So we built the tech, and put an expert team behind it, to get the right venue, the right price and a real plan in front of you in minutes instead of days, whatever the scale of the event.

Hire Space is the intelligent venue platform for business events. Not a marketplace. Not a directory. A tech-powered venue-finding platform with an expert team behind it.

Our audiences

Who we're writing for

Four archetypes cover most of our audience. The tone shifts for each, the rules don't.

The Strategist

Head of Events, Events Director

"I need a partner that helps my team work smarter, not another agency telling me how to run events."

Owns: strategy, budget, the team, reporting ROI to the board.

Tone: peer-level. Respect their expertise. Lead with data, benchmarking and leverage. Never explain their own job.

The Coordinator

Events Manager, SME Events Lead

"I just need to find a good venue, fast, and not screw it up."

Owns: day-to-day execution, under pressure, minimal admin tolerance.

Tone: helpful, specific, energetic. Speed matters. Make them look good to their boss.

The Accidental Booker

PA, Office Manager, HR, social committee

"I'm not an events person, I've just been asked to sort the team away day."

Owns: one to a handful of events a year. Wants guidance and reassurance.

Tone: warm, explanatory, zero jargon. Lean on ease and the human team behind the tech.

The Buyer

Procurement, Finance, Commercial

"Show me the consolidated contract, the spend data and the risk profile."

Owns: approval, supplier rationalisation, audit, compliance.

Tone: measured, credential-led, factual. Lead with consolidation, T&Cs, spend visibility.

Our brand voice

Informative but informal, warm and never corporate

We write like we’re explaining something to a colleague over coffee, not presenting at a board meeting. Our sentences are usually short and active, but we mix in longer ones when a thought needs room to breathe. Details are concrete: named venues, real numbers, what actually happened last Tuesday rather than "a memorable afternoon of engagement". We lean on experience over opinion: "I had great feedback from the Palm Court last October" beats "I think the Palm Court would be great".

How we show up

1. Industry thought-leaders

Useful advice our audience actually wants to read.

2. Champions of the best venues

Whether they're new, unique or just the most suitable.

3. Friendly and approachable

It's about building relationships. The tone stays friendly and human.

4. Confident, specific, fast

Never a brochure. Never a board-room memo. Never a sales pitch for its own sake.

Do

  • Varied sentence lengths, active voice, British English
  • Contractions by default (we're, it's, you'll)
  • Concrete details: "The Palm Court opened in 1927"
  • Name people, venues and specifics
  • "and" not "&" (unless in a venue name)

Don't

  • Jargon or stacked promotional adjectives
  • Filler triplets ("creativity, collaboration and connection")
  • Fake attributions ("experts say")
  • Generic conclusions ("the future looks bright")
  • Synonym cycling ("venue" → "space" → "spot")

AI writing rules

Prompting LLM Models

These rules are how we keep AI-written Hire Space copy sounding like us and not like everyone else's AI. Bake them into the prompt, the system message or the custom instructions in whichever tool you're using. They apply to any piece of customer-facing copy an AI helps you draft: marketing, blog, newsletter, social, proposals, tenders, ad copy, venue descriptions.

No dashes

No em dashes (—). No en dashes (–). Use commas, full stops, parentheses or "to" for ranges ("Monday to Friday", "10 to 20 guests"). Hyphens (-) are fine for compound adjectives.

No Oxford commas

Drop the comma before "and" or "or" in lists. "Venues, caterers and suppliers", not "venues, caterers, and suppliers". If ambiguity results, restructure the sentence.

No sycophancy

No "Great question!", "Hope this helps!", "Let me know if you need anything else!". Cut stacked hedges. "In order to" becomes "to".

British English

Organise, colour, programme, favourite, optimise, realise, centre, travelling. Tell the model you want British spelling and it will stop defaulting to US.

Banned AI-tell vocabulary

  • delve / dive into
  • let's dive in / explore
  • testament
  • tapestry
  • landscape (figurative)
  • nestled

Emoji in practice

When emoji help and when they hurt

The rule is simple: emoji belong where conversation happens and nowhere else. Use them as accents, not punctuation.

Emoji: OK

  • Social captions (LinkedIn, Instagram, Facebook, X, TikTok)
  • Slack and other internal chat
  • Subject lines, sparingly, when the tone of the campaign invites it

Emoji: NOT OK

  • Blog body text, newsletter body, email signatures
  • Venue descriptions, meta descriptions, GMB posts
  • Ad copy, press releases, sales collateral
  • Tender responses and proposals

Brand emoji

The Hire Space brand emoji is 💚 (the green heart). Use it at sign-offs and sparingly in body. Never stack emojis as punctuation and never use emoji as a substitute for a verb.

Logo

The Hire Space mark

Our logo is a simple wordmark paired with the house icon. The full-colour version lives on white or very pale backgrounds. On dark or saturated backgrounds, reach for the white monochrome lockup. Never edit, distort, recolour, re-space or reconfigure it, treat it as a single unit.

The icon on its own

The house-in-circle icon can stand alone where the full lockup would be too wide or too detailed to read. Think favicons, app icons, social profile pictures, small UI elements, watermarks and any context where the wordmark has already been established.

Elevation

Three levels of shadow, nothing else

Shadow is how we show depth without using a border. We have three elevation levels. Pick the one that fits the job, use it consistently, and don’t invent a fourth.

Elevation 01
Subtle
0 2px 8px rgba(12,19,24,0.06)

For hover states, input focus rings, very small UI elements and inline cards where you want just a hint of lift.

Elevation 02
Medium
0 10px 28px rgba(12,19,24,0.09)

The default for cards, callouts and panels that need to feel like they’re sitting above the page. Use this most of the time.

Elevation 03
Elevated
0 22px 60px rgba(12,19,24,0.14)

Reserved for hero elements. Title pages, landing page heroes, big feature blocks you want readers to notice first.

House rules

  • Never mix two elevation levels on the same surface.
  • Don’t combine a shadow with a visible border. Pick one or the other.
  • Shadows use rgba(12, 19, 24, x) — our primary black — never pure #000.
  • On dark surfaces, skip the shadow. Use a subtle background-lightness change instead.

Photography

Professional, but informal, light-hearted and natural

A distinctive photographic style built around the philosophy of creating "amazing experiences". Our imagery should reflect the energy of the events industry and what it's like to work with Hire Space.

It should support our values: informative but informal, forward-thinking and approachable.

In practice, that means candid, non-posed shots and natural light where possible. Real people, real venues, real moments.

Iconography

Bold icons, sometimes in a circle

Icons are how we signal at a glance. They support a headline or a stat, they don’t try to carry the content themselves.

House rules:

  • Use Font Awesome Solid as the default set.
  • Dark ink for inline and navigation.
  • White on a green circle for stats, feature call-outs or key moments.
  • In bulleted lists, stick to ticks and crosses in a circle, small green dots or a plain green arrow. Don’t mix different icons per line.
  • Stick to one treatment per page, don’t mix them up.
